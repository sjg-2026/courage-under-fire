# Background

Context for the source material behind this dashboard, the people in it, and the editorial choices made in this **Leadership Edition**.

## The author

**Vice Admiral James Bond Stockdale (1923–2005)** was a United States Navy aviator and one of the most highly decorated officers in the Navy's history. Before his combat career he studied at Stanford University, where — almost by accident — he discovered philosophy. He was shot down on a combat mission in September 1965 and held as a prisoner of war for seven and a half years, four of them in solitary confinement. As the senior ranking officer among the prisoners, he organized and led a clandestine community of fellow captives. He was awarded the Medal of Honor for his leadership in captivity, and later became president of the Naval War College and a fellow at Stanford's Hoover Institution.

## The speech

*Courage Under Fire: Testing Epictetus's Doctrines in a Laboratory of Human Behavior* was delivered at the Great Hall, King's College London, on **November 15, 1993**, and published by the Hoover Institution. In it, Stockdale recounts how the Stoic philosophy he learned at Stanford became the practical framework he relied on to survive captivity with his integrity intact. He frames his imprisonment as a "laboratory" in which the doctrines of Epictetus were put to the most demanding possible test — and, in his verdict, "passed with flying colors."

## The philosophy: Stoicism and Epictetus

**Stoicism** is a school of philosophy founded in Athens in the early 3rd century BC. Its central claim is that the good life comes from virtue and from focusing only on what is within our control — our own judgments, choices, and responses — while accepting everything external with equanimity.

**Epictetus (c. AD 50–135)** was born into slavery and later freed in Rome, becoming one of the most influential Stoic teachers. He left no writings of his own; his teachings survive through notes taken by his student Arrian, collected as the *Discourses* and the *Enchiridion* (Greek for "ready at hand" — a handbook). Stockdale carried the *Enchiridion* into combat and into prison.

### The dichotomy of control

The single most important idea Stockdale drew on is Epictetus's **dichotomy of control**: dividing everything into two categories — what is "up to us" (our opinions, aims, aversions, and above all our own good and evil) and what is "not up to us" (our body, reputation, property, station in life, and ultimately our fate). Anxiety and loss of self come from coveting what is not ours to control. Freedom and serenity come from investing fully in what is.

### Shame, not pain

A recurring theme — and the emotional core of the speech — is Stockdale's discovery that what truly breaks a person is not physical pain but **shame**: the feeling of having betrayed oneself and one's comrades. His leadership in captivity was built around denying his captors the ability to impose that shame.

## BACK US

To give his fellow prisoners clear, memorable rules that could hold up under extreme pressure, Stockdale issued a set of orders summarized by the acronym **BACK US**:

- **B** — Don't **Bow** in public
- **A** — Stay off the **Air** (propaganda broadcasts)
- **C** — Admit no **Crimes**
- **K** — Never **Kiss** them goodbye (don't accept early release as a reward for compliance)
- **US** — officially "United States," but Stockdale meant **Unity over Self**

The genius of BACK US was choosing a small number of bright-line refusals everyone could rally around, so prisoners could comply on minor demands without shame while resisting together on what mattered. The dashboard's reflection section maps each letter to a modern organizational principle (leadership, strategy, decision-making, team dynamics, and culture).

## The Stockdale Paradox

Years later, in Jim Collins's book *Good to Great* (2001), Stockdale's outlook was named the **Stockdale Paradox**: the discipline to *confront the most brutal facts of your current reality, whatever they might be, and at the same time never lose faith that you will prevail in the end.* Collins recounts that the prisoners who fared worst were the optimists who fixed on a date ("we'll be out by Christmas") and were repeatedly crushed when it passed. Stockdale's posture — unflinching realism paired with unshakable resolve — is the strategic backbone of the reflection section in this dashboard.

## Invictus

The speech ends with the final stanza of **"Invictus"** by the English poet William Ernest Henley (1849–1903):

> It matters not how strait the gate,
> How charged with punishments the scroll,
> I am the master of my fate:
> I am the captain of my soul.

In the original account, a fellow prisoner passed this verse to Stockdale on a smuggled note after a long period of isolation. It captures the whole of his message: external circumstances may be beyond our control, but the disposition of our own will is not.

## Editorial note on this edition

This **Leadership Edition** was adapted from a fuller summary for broad, general-audience distribution. The following changes were made deliberately:

- **Graphic content removed or softened.** Detailed descriptions of torture techniques, physical injuries, and a self-harm episode in the original speech were trimmed or rephrased.
- **Nationalities generalized.** Specific references to the captor nation and city were replaced with neutral terms ("the captors," "the camp") so the piece reads as a leadership artifact rather than a depiction of a particular people.
- **Tone neutralized.** Colloquial and politically charged phrasing was replaced with professional, inclusive language; a comparison to a religious text was reframed as neutral historical context.

These adaptations move some wording away from Stockdale's original text. Readers who want the unaltered account should consult the [original essay (PDF)](https://www.hoover.org/sites/default/files/uploads/documents/StockdaleCourage.pdf).
